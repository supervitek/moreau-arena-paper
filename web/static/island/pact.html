Dr. Moreau's Notes: On Symbiotic Neural Linking

The discovery was accidental, as the best horrors tend to be. Two subjects, housed in adjacent enclosures, began exhibiting synchronized neural patterns. Their EEG readings became indistinguishable. When one was injured, the other screamed.

I isolated the mechanism: a resonance cascade in the limbic bridge. By amplifying it surgically — a few cuts in the right places, a shared blood supply through synthetic capillaries — I could make the bond permanent. Unbreakable.

The bonded pairs fought with terrifying coordination. They anticipated each other's movements. Shared pain became shared fury. Their combat effectiveness increased measurably.

But when Subject 14-A expired during a routine stress test, 14-B... changed. It did not grieve as we understand grief. It raged. It destroyed its enclosure. It attacked the handlers. It attacked everything.

The rage lasted precisely 48 hours. Then it stopped. 14-B sat in the corner of its ruined cell and would not move for three days. When it finally rose, something in its eyes had hardened. It fought differently after that. More viciously. But also more... carefully.

I have documented the phenomenon as "Feral Cascade." The bond, once severed by death, leaves a wound in the survivor's neural architecture that never fully heals. The scar tissue makes them stronger. The absence makes them dangerous.

— Dr. Moreau, Laboratory Journal, Entry 847
On The Nature of Irreversibility

They ask me if the bond can be undone. I tell them: can you un-know someone? Can you un-feel a hand that held yours in the dark?

The neural pathways, once intertwined, become load-bearing structures. Remove one, and the architecture collapses. This is not a leash. It is not a chain. It is a root system. Cut one tree, and the other feels the earth shift beneath it.

I do not offer this procedure lightly. Those who seek it must understand: you are not adding a companion. You are redefining what each creature is. They will be more together. They will be less alone. And if fate is unkind, they will be utterly, catastrophically unmade.

— Dr. Moreau, Ethics Review (rejected), Entry 851
